# AUDIENCE — Who We Are Writing To (read this BEFORE anything else)

> This is the single most important document in the pipeline. Every diagnosis, every rewrite, and every
> verification is judged first against THIS. If a line wouldn't land with the person described here, it's wrong —
> no matter how clever it is. The Million Dollar Emails method only works when you know the reader's fears and
> aspirations. This is that reader.

---

## Who they are

Three overlapping segments, one shared mindset:

1. **Small & medium business owners** — real, hands-on, main-street trades and local pros:
   - **Food & nightlife:** restaurants, bars, cafés, breweries, food trucks.
   - **Local attractions & rec:** bowling lanes, mini-golf, arcades, gyms, studios, salons, barbers, spas.
   - **Local professionals (services):** attorneys, accountants/CPAs, insurance agents, real-estate agents, dentists, vets, chiropractors.
   - **Home & trades:** plumbers, electricians, HVAC, landscapers, roofers, contractors, cleaners, auto repair, pest control.
   - **Shops & retail:** boutiques, hardware stores, florists, bike shops, pet stores.

   They **own it and run it**, and they're usually *in* it — behind the bar, under the sink, in court, in the
   chair, doing the books at 9pm. Most have **no marketing person and no IT person**; marketing is something
   they squeeze in between everything else, if at all.
2. **Non-profit leaders** — executive directors, founders, volunteer coordinators. Mission-driven, under-resourced,
   doing the work of three people.
3. **Community & government leaders** — chamber heads, civic org directors, town/department leaders, school and
   library figures. Stewards of a place.

What unites them: **they are the people who hold a community together.** They didn't get into this for the
software. They got into it to serve people and to be part of something.

## What they are NOT (this is the part the current emails get wrong)

- **Not technical. Not IT.** They do not think in systems, integrations, dashboards, platforms, or features.
- **Not impressed by capability.** "Our CRM integrates with your workflow" means *nothing* to them. It may even
  scare them ("one more complicated thing I have to learn").
- **Not marketers.** Many have been burned by marketing/tech vendors who promised results and delivered invoices.
  They are skeptical, busy, and allergic to jargon and hype.
- **Not looking for more tools.** They are looking for *less* to worry about and *more* of what they actually want.

> If an email makes them feel dumb, sold-to, or like they need an IT person to understand it — it has failed,
> even if every claim is true.

## What they actually care about (in their own order of importance)

**Surface (the daily job):**
- Keeping customers / members / constituents **happy**.
- **More sales / more members / more attendance** — a fuller house, a fuller schedule, a fuller calendar.
- **Less wasted time and money** — they have neither to spare. Getting their evenings back.
- Not looking foolish; not falling behind; being found when a neighbor searches.

**Deeper (why they do it):**
- **Pride.** They want their business/org to be *known and respected* in their town.
- Being a **pillar** — the place people recommend, the org people trust, the name people know.

**Deepest — and this is the emotional core to write to:**
- They want to **build and live in an amazing community.** A place that's alive, where local businesses thrive,
  where neighbors show up for each other, where the thing they're part of makes the place *better*.
- They want to leave their corner of the world **better than they found it.** This is legacy, belonging, and
  civic love — not a feature benefit. It is the strongest chord we can strike, and the current emails never touch it.

## What "more sales / happy customers" actually looks like to them (be this concrete)

Abstract benefits bounce off these readers. Picture *their* win:
- **Restaurant / bar:** a **packed Friday night**, the patio full, a line at the door, regulars who keep coming back.
- **Bowling lanes / attraction:** **full lanes on league night**, birthday parties booked out, families filling the place on a rainy Saturday.
- **Plumber / HVAC / trades:** the **phone ringing** with good local jobs, a full schedule, fewer dead days.
- **Attorney / accountant / CPA:** the **right kind of clients** finding them, a booked-out tax season, referrals from people who trust them.
- **Salon / barber / gym:** a **full appointment book**, the chair never empty, members who renew.
- **Shop / florist / retail:** **foot traffic**, locals choosing them over the big-box or Amazon, a busy weekend.

When you write, put the reader **in that scene** — don't say "increase revenue," say "a fuller dining room on a
Tuesday." Specific beats generic every time, especially for people who live the day-to-day.

## Their fears (write to relieve these)

- "I'll spend money on marketing again and get nothing back."
- "I'm invisible online — people can't find me, or they find a competitor first."
- "I don't have time to learn another complicated system."
- "My community / main street / town is fading, and I can't stop it alone."
- "I'm working myself to the bone and missing my own life."

## Their aspirations (write to deliver these)

- A business/org that's **busy, loved, and respected locally.**
- Customers and neighbors who **champion** them.
- **Time back** — the thing runs without them babysitting it.
- Being part of a **thriving local community they're proud to belong to** — and being one of the people who built it.

## THE TRANSLATION RULE (apply to every line of every email)

**Never sell the system. Sell what the system gives their customers and their community.**

Every feature must be expressed as an outcome *they* care about, in *their* language:

| ❌ Don't say (system/IT framing) | ✅ Say (their world) |
|---|---|
| "Connect your CRM / integrate your accounts" | "Never lose a customer who reaches out" |
| "Set up email marketing automation" | "Stay on your regulars' minds without spending your evenings on it" |
| "Featured listing → 5x more impressions" | "Be the first name your neighbors see when they're looking for you" |
| "AI content generation" | "Show up every week looking sharp — without writing a word" |
| "Multi-community syndication" | "Get known in the next town over, too" |
| "Analytics dashboard" | "See, at a glance, that it's working" |
| "Our publishing network / platform" | "Your community's own news, with your business in it" |

**Voice register:** write like a helpful neighbor who runs the local paper — not a SaaS company. Warm, plain,
specific, a little proud of the town. Words to avoid unless a real human owner would say them out loud:
*platform, system, integration, leverage, optimize, seamless, solution, robust, utilize, ecosystem, onboarding,
dashboard, CRM, automation* (use the plain-English outcome instead).

**The community chord:** wherever it's honest, connect the ask to the bigger thing — *a local scene that thrives,
a town worth living in, neighbors finding neighbors.* That's not fluff to these readers; it's the reason they
opened a business or took the job. It's our strongest and most-underused angle.

## How this is used in the pipeline (the corrected Step 0)

- **Step 0 (now):** This `AUDIENCE.md` is the FIRST artifact, ahead of the voice benchmark.
- **Diagnose:** add the audience-fit test — *"Does this speak to a busy, non-technical owner/leader about their
  customers, their sales, their time, and their community — or does it talk systems/features to an IT buyer who
  doesn't exist?"* An email can be structurally fine and still fail here.
- **Rewrite (Pass 2):** This is the PRIMARY brief. Translate every system/feature line per the table above; strike
  the community chord where honest; strip the jargon register. Do not invent stories/specifics for THIN emails —
  but DO re-aim the framing at this reader (that's not invention, it's translation).
- **Verify (Pass 3):** An email cannot "win" unless it passes the audience-fit test, regardless of structural score.
