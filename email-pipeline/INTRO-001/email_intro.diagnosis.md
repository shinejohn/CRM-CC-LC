# INTRO-001 / email_intro — Diagnosis
SCORE: 3/5
THIN: no
Ask (single? what is it): Yes — "Claim your listing" is the single primary CTA (free, 5 min). Clean for an intro; the only one in the batch with one clear ask.
Lesson (present? state it): Present and good for first-touch: "your community already has a real local media network (newspaper + calendar + directory + sites + newsletter), and you can be in it." Two-level: useful awareness + setup for the paid network.
Story/angle: A-to-B but with one genuinely good beat — "No other organization does all of this for [Community Name]. Not Facebook. Not Google. Not Yelp. Us." That contrast is the strongest line in the batch and earns attention.
Opening: throat-clearing — "I'm [AM Name], your account manager at [Community Name] Day.News." Standard intro self-ID; acceptable for a true first-touch but not concrete-from-word-one.
Subject verdict: ~83 chars (too long — flag); describe-leaning but has a mild "here's what that means for you" hook; no spam triggers; human, slightly statement-y.
Preview verdict: repeats — "Your community's newspaper event calendar and business directory" just restates the subject's network claim; doesn't do a second job.
CTA count: 1 primary (claim) — strong; reply/call mentioned as soft option, acceptable.
Hold-back / click-driver: ok — it explains the network but the "claim your listing" payoff and slot specifics give a real reason to click; leaves them slightly curious about their own slot count.
Hero: mixed-to-product — heavy on what Day.News IS (five bullets about the network); reader's business enters late. For an intro this is tolerable but could center the reader sooner.
Voice: human — the "Not Facebook. Not Google. Not Yelp. Us." cadence is genuinely human and confident.
Top 3 problems to fix (specific, actionable): 1) Cut the subject to ≤~50 chars and open a curiosity loop (lead with the "nobody else does all of this" angle). 2) Rewrite preview to do a second job (e.g., finish the thought / tease the slot scarcity) instead of restating the network list. 3) Trim the five-bullet network catalog and get the reader's business into the first third so they're the hero sooner.
One-line overall: A solid first-touch with one clean ask and a genuinely human "not Facebook/Google/Yelp" hook, dragged down by an over-long subject, a repeating preview, and too much product-as-hero framing.
